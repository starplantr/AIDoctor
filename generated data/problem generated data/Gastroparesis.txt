**Case 1:**

A 58-year-old woman with a history of type 2 diabetes presents to the clinic with complaints of nausea and vomiting of undigested food for the past several weeks. She reports feeling full even after eating small amounts and has unintentionally lost 10 pounds over the last month. 

*What is the most likely diagnosis of the patient?*

The most likely diagnosis for this patient is **gastroparesis**. Her symptoms of nausea, vomiting of undigested food, early satiety, and unintentional weight loss, in the context of a history of diabetes, strongly suggest this diagnosis. Gastroparesis is common in diabetic patients due to the neuropathy that can occur in the vagus nerve, which controls the stomach muscles.

She also mentions feeling full after eating only a small amount of food and has experienced recent fluctuations in her blood glucose levels despite no changes in diet or medication.

*What is the most likely diagnosis of the patient?*

The most likely diagnosis for this patient is **gastroparesis**. Her symptoms of chronic nausea, abdominal bloating, early satiety, and fluctuating blood glucose levels, in conjunction with her history of hypothyroidism and possibly the usage of certain types of antidepressants, suggest this diagnosis. Both hypothyroidism and certain antidepressants are known to be associated with gastroparesis.

**Case 5:**

*What is the most likely diagnosis of the patient?*

The most likely diagnosis for this patient is **gastroparesis**. His symptoms of chronic nausea, early satiety, and heartburn, along with a medical history of Parkinson's disease, suggest this condition. Parkinson's disease is a neurodegenerative disorder that can also affect the autonomic nervous system, including the vagus nerve, leading to gastroparesis.
