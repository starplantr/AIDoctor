**Case 1:**

A 75-year-old male presents to the clinic with a 2-month history of early satiety and persistent vomiting of undigested food. He reports a significant loss of appetite and unintentional weight loss. His past medical history reveals a diagnosis of peptic ulcer disease for which he has been taking proton pump inhibitors. 

*What is the most likely diagnosis of the patient?*

Given the patient's symptoms of early satiety, vomiting of undigested food, and weight loss, along with a history of peptic ulcer disease, the most likely diagnosis is **gastric outlet obstruction (GOO)**. Peptic ulcers are one of the most common benign causes of GOO, especially in older adults.

**Case 2:**

A 65-year-old woman comes to the clinic with recurrent nausea and vomiting. She reports feeling full after eating only a small amount of food. She has also lost weight and complains of feeling weak. Her medical history includes a diagnosis of gastric cancer two years ago. She underwent surgery and has been on chemotherapy since then.

*What is the most likely diagnosis of the patient?*

The patient's symptoms of persistent nausea, vomiting, early satiety, weight loss, and a history of gastric cancer suggest the diagnosis of **gastric outlet obstruction (GOO)**. Gastric cancer is a known malignant cause of GOO and could have recurred or led to scar tissue resulting in the obstruction.

**Case 3:**

A 55-year-old man is brought to the emergency department with severe abdominal pain, vomiting, and visible dehydration. The patient's wife states that he has been complaining of feeling full after eating only small amounts of food and has been losing weight over the past several weeks. His medical history includes chronic pancreatitis.

*What is the most likely diagnosis of the patient?*

Considering the patient's chronic pancreatitis history and his symptoms of abdominal pain, vomiting, visible dehydration, early satiety, and weight loss, the most probable diagnosis is **gastric outlet obstruction (GOO)**. Chronic pancreatitis can result in fibrosis and inflammation, potentially causing extrinsic compression on the duodenum, leading to GOO.
