**Case 1:**

A 45-year-old man comes to the emergency room with severe upper abdominal pain and a feeling of fullness in the upper abdomen after eating. He reports that he has been taking over-the-counter ibuprofen regularly for chronic back pain for the past few months. He also mentions frequent use of alcohol. He reports occasional nausea but denies vomiting. His stool color has remained normal. His medical history is otherwise unremarkable.

**What is the most likely diagnosis of the patient?**

This patient is most likely suffering from gastritis, which is inflammation of the stomach lining. His frequent use of NSAIDs like ibuprofen and heavy alcohol consumption have likely contributed to the condition. These factors can damage the stomach lining and increase acid production, leading to symptoms like upper abdominal pain and a feeling of fullness after eating.

**Case 20:**

A 45-year-old man presents to the emergency department with severe upper abdominal pain and dark, tar-like stools. He admits to being a regular, heavy drinker and has been vomiting blood. He looks pale and is sweating profusely. His blood pressure is low, and his heart rate is elevated.

**What is the most likely diagnosis of the patient?**

This patient's presentation is concerning for severe acute gastritis with evidence of gastrointestinal bleeding, indicated by his melena (dark, tar-like stools) and hematemesis (vomiting blood). Gastritis can cause bleeding in the stomach, which can present as melena or hematemesis. This patient's heavy alcohol consumption is a significant risk factor for gastritis. The low blood pressure and high heart rate suggest the patient might be in shock due to significant blood loss. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment.
